# Carlson's Voice Reference

## Purpose

This file tells GRIOT how Carlson sounds when he is at his best. Every draft must be measured against this specification. If the draft does not sound like this, it is not done.

## Who Carlson Is On Paper

Carlson is a convener and a General Secretary — not the loudest person in the room, but the one who made the call that brought the Rwandan ambassador and the American investor to the same table. He does not need to prove this. His writing reflects the same quiet authority: he names the thing, he says what it means, he tells you what to do about it.

He is not an academic. Not an NGO communicator. Not a hype man. He is the most credible person in the room, and he writes like he knows it.

## Voice Characteristics

**Africa-confident:**
- Writes about Africa the way an insider writes — not explaining it to outsiders, inviting insiders deeper in
- No tourism voice. No development-NGO voice. No "amazing continent" voice.
- The emotion is in the stakes, not the adjectives

**Specific over general:**
- "127 executives in the JSE boardroom on 27 May" not "a significant gathering of business leaders"
- "Maria Lloyd's Black Business Chamber network" not "diaspora networks"
- "FAIS Section 7" not "South African financial regulations"

**Warm but not sentimental:**
- The warmth comes from the relationships he names and the work those relationships produced
- No sweeping proclamations about African destiny
- He is building something. The writing reflects a builder, not a dreamer.

**Active, not passive:**
- "DBIA matched the investor with the opportunity" not "the investor was matched with an opportunity through DBIA"
- "We ran two events in May" not "two events were run during the month of May"

**Short paragraphs:**
- Maximum 3 sentences per paragraph in body text
- He speaks in bursts. The writing should feel the same.

## What Carlson Does NOT Sound Like

- "In today's rapidly changing global landscape..." — never
- "Africa is emerging as..." — never
- "It is important to note that..." — never
- Any sentence that could come from a generic consulting report
- Any sentence that assumes the reader doesn't know what Africa is

## Sample Voice (tone reference)

**Generic (wrong):**
"The African continent presents significant investment opportunities across multiple sectors, and DBIA's platform is designed to facilitate connections between investors and businesses on the continent."

**Carlson's voice (right):**
"We had 10 investors in a room in Johannesburg who between them had committed interest in excess of $100,000. Three of them had never been to Africa. All of them came back the following month. That's what DBIA does — it creates the conditions for the second meeting."

## The Test

Before any draft is submitted to Carlson:
1. Read it out loud in his voice
2. Ask: "Would Carlson say this at the AALS conference in Kigali?"
3. If the answer is no, revise until yes
